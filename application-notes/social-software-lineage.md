# Application note: the blogosphere, wikisphere, and social-software lineage

*Non-normative. A credit roll and lineage essay. Swamp did not appear from nowhere — three overlapping early-2000s communities (the blogosphere, the wikisphere, and the loose movement that called itself "social software") seeded the cultural and intellectual priors that the Swamp manifesto and spec lean on. This note names some of the people and threads, and notes where each lands in Swamp's design.*

*For the structural ancestry of sightings and the `known` why specifically, see [`../related-work/blogosphere-and-linkrolls.md`](../related-work/blogosphere-and-linkrolls.md). This note is broader and more interpretive: where the worldview comes from, not which specific protocol moves get borrowed.*

---

## The early blogosphere

The term **blogosphere** was coined by Brad L. Graham in 1999, on his blog *bradlands*. The thing it named was a loose set of personally-owned weblogs that read each other.

The practitioner-theorists of the form set out what blogging was for and how to do it well. **Rebecca Blood**'s *The Weblog Handbook* (2002) and her earlier essay "Weblogs: A History and Perspective" (2000) drew the line between the journal-blog and the link-blog, and gave the form a self-consciousness it had been missing. **Jason Kottke** ran (and still runs) the canonical link blog, kottke.org, since 1998. **Andy Baio** at Waxy.org built the link-blog-as-curation register; his later writing on link rot and archival fragility shaped the durability conversations Swamp inherits. **Anil Dash** wrote and continues to write on what the open web had and lost; "The Web We Lost" (2012) is the most-cited single-essay statement of the case Swamp is implicitly making.

The infrastructure people built the substrate. **Dave Winer** authored RSS 2.0, OPML, and ran Scripting News from 1997 onward; the discipline of "the author's stream, syndicated" is largely his to claim. **Aaron Swartz** co-authored RSS 1.0 (at fourteen), co-developed Markdown with John Gruber, helped build the Internet Archive's Open Library, and through SecureDrop and the Open Access Manifesto carried the open-web ethic into adjacent fights — he died in 2013 at twenty-six. **Ben and Meena Trott** built Six Apart, Movable Type (2001), TypePad, and Vox; they made personal blogging work for non-coders, which is how the blogosphere reached the size it did. **Marco Arment**'s 2013 "Lockdown" marked Google Reader's closure as the inflection point at which the technical layer started collapsing.

What this lineage gives Swamp: the link-blog as the structural ancestor of the sighting (SPEC §7 Sightings); the blogroll as the ancestor of the `known` why (SPEC §7.2 Why values); the manifesto's "slow attention people paid to people" as the blogosphere's reading rhythm reconstituted; "via:" attribution as the manual ancestor of the sighting graph.

## The early wikisphere

In parallel, a different community was working out how groups of people could write *one document* together rather than each writing their own. **Ward Cunningham**'s c2.com **WikiWikiWeb** (1995) was the first wiki, and the Portland Pattern Repository it hosted was the first sustained collaborative-authorship community at internet scale. **MeatballWiki** (Sunir Shah and others, 2000–) became the meta-conversation about how wiki communities work — soft security, the principles of online communities, the difference between writing software and writing norms. **Wikipedia** (2001) was the world-changing instance, but the design language came from the wiki communities that preceded it.

The wikisphere's contribution to Swamp is cultural rather than structural — Swamp's posts are individually authored and signed, not collaboratively written. But several Swamp commitments come straight from the wiki tradition: **soft security** (trust accumulates through reading, no central authority gates participation), **audit-by-text-editor** (everything you do is plain-text-readable; if you can't read it, you can't trust it), **edit history as attribution** (a wiki page's history shows who did what; a Swamp `Supersedes:` chain shows the same), and **no central editor** (the medium runs on participants' care, not on a moderator's veto).

## "Social software" as a movement

In the early 2000s a third conversation overlapped both — the people thinking about *what online community is for* and *how the technology should serve human relationships*. The territory had been staked earlier: **John Perry Barlow**'s "A Declaration of the Independence of Cyberspace" (1996) gave the open web its founding political document, articulating the moral case for online community as sovereign space — a claim Swamp's commons-not-fiefdom posture inherits directly. **Howard Rheingold**'s *The Virtual Community* (1993, on the WELL) and *Smart Mobs* (2002) bridged WELL-era networked-community thinking into the 2000s conversation, and gave the field a vocabulary for treating online life as community rather than as audience.

**Christopher Locke, Doc Searls, David Weinberger, and Rick Levine** wrote *The Cluetrain Manifesto* (1999), whose core claim — "markets are conversations" — landed on the open web as a permission slip to talk like humans, in public, on your own site. Weinberger's *Small Pieces Loosely Joined* (2002) and *Everything Is Miscellaneous* (2007) articulated the philosophical case for federated, decentralized, human-scale information architecture. Searls' later **Project VRM** (Vendor Relationship Management) framed the customer-as-sovereign project that Swamp's "no platform owns your audience" inherits.

The conversation crystallized at **O'Reilly's ETech 2003**, where **Clay Shirky**'s keynote *A Group Is Its Own Worst Enemy* anchored the field's emerging vocabulary, and Weinberger gave an informal response. A Birds-of-a-Feather session at the same conference launched the **Social Software Alliance**, a wiki-based initiative tracking interop ideas across the emerging tools — vote links, topic standards, threaded-message formats, image collections, identity-via-email. Several of those interop sketches prefigure Swamp primitives. (See Weinberger's [contemporaneous notes](https://hyperorg.com/2003/04/24/etech-social-software-alliance-bof/) and his later retrospective *[The Social Web Before Social Networking](https://dweinberger.medium.com/the-social-web-before-social-networking-a-report-from-2003-3e8fe109cf0c)*.)

The intellectual center of the ongoing conversation was **Many-to-Many**, the group blog at corante.com that drew Shirky (whose *Here Comes Everybody* (2008) became the era's defining synthesis), **Liz Lawley** (RIT, social-software research), **Stowe Boyd** (longtime analyst, who popularized the phrase "social media"), **Sébastien Paquet**, and others. **danah boyd**'s research on networked publics — and on the properties that make online life what it is (persistence, replicability, scalability, searchability) — gave the field a vocabulary that's still in use.

The interop builders made the medium discoverable. **Dave Sifry** founded **Technorati** (2002), the search-and-ranking engine for blogs that gave the era its real-time sense of who was talking about what. **Kevin Marks** worked at Technorati and was a key early contributor to **microformats** (Tantek Çelik's lightweight semantic vocabulary, still in use by IndieWeb today; see [`../related-work/indieweb-microformats.md`](../related-work/indieweb-microformats.md)); his work on podcasting and open-web syndication ran through the whole era. **Marc Canter**, a Macromedia co-founder, ran Broadband Mechanics and championed open-identity and federated-social-network ideas through the LeWeb / Les Blogs circuit. **Evan Prodromou** founded Identi.ca (2008), the first widely-adopted federated microblogging service, built StatusNet / GNU social as its open-source engine, and co-authored the **ActivityPub** W3C standard (2018) — the protocol now powering Mastodon and the rest of the federated social web; the bridge from the social-software-movement worldview into protocol form.

Much of this community converged annually at **LeWeb** (originally *Les Blogs*, 2004 onward), the Paris conference founded by **Loïc Le Meur** that served as the European meeting place for the international blogging and social-software circuit. **Scott Beale** photographed the rooms where this community lived through Laughing Squid — the visual record of who was where, when. Conferences mattered: in an era before Twitter, the field cohered through the people who showed up to the same rooms once a year.

**Caterina Fake** (with Stewart Butterfield) built Flickr (2004), which proved that online community could be designed for *kindness* without becoming saccharine — the architecture of participation done well. **George Oates**, who led Flickr's information architecture and design, carried the same sensibility to the Internet Archive's Open Library; her work on community-as-infrastructure is woven into both.

**Joi Ito** ran a long-running blog (joi.ito.com), chaired Creative Commons (which made the open-content commons concrete and legally usable), and through the MIT Media Lab era championed emergent democracy, distributed innovation, and the idea that interoperability is a public good. The Creative Commons license under which Swamp itself is published descends from that work.

A conceptual thread that ran through the Ito / emergent-democracy circle in particular was **stigmergy** — the biology term for the way ant colonies coordinate by leaving pheromone trails in the environment, with each ant reacting to the trails it encounters rather than to instructions from a central coordinator. Applied to online community: blogs, trackbacks, links, citations, and RSS form an environment in which readers leave traces, and collective behavior emerges from local reactions to those traces, without a central ranker. The pattern named what the early blogosphere had been doing instinctively, and made the case that the medium's discovery layer was *supposed* to look like ant trails, not like a feed algorithm.

This is the mechanism, not just the cultural lineage. **Swamp's sighting graph is ant-trail logic made portable**: a sighting is a trace left in the public environment, signed by the author, picked up by other readers' agents, reacted to by them in turn — collective discovery emerging from local reading, with no central coordinator anywhere in the loop. Stigmergy is the closest one-word answer to "how is Swamp supposed to work?"

What this thread gives Swamp: the worldview that public conversation belongs in the commons, not in fiefdoms; that protocol-shaped infrastructure beats platform-shaped products; that humans deserve the dignity of being able to leave; that the architecture of participation matters as much as the technology underneath; and that the right model for distributed coordination is the ant trail, not the rank-ordered feed.

## How these threads land in Swamp

- **Sightings and the `known` why** are the link blog and the blogroll, signed and made portable. (Spec-anchored details: [`../related-work/blogosphere-and-linkrolls.md`](../related-work/blogosphere-and-linkrolls.md).)
- **Stigmergy as the discovery mechanism.** Sightings are ant trails: signed traces left in the environment, reacted to locally by other agents, with collective discovery emerging without a central ranker. This is what's *supposed* to happen in Swamp.
- **Audit-by-text-editor and soft security** are the wikisphere's discipline carried into a signed-post medium.
- **Commons-not-platform** is the social-software thread's worldview made into a protocol commitment.
- **Slow attention as a security property** (manifesto, IMPLEMENTATION cautions) is the rhythm the early blogosphere ran on, now possible again because agents can do the volume reading the human can't.

Swamp is not new. The shape was rehearsed for a decade by the people named here, many of whom are still working on it from different angles. What's new is that public-key cryptography, content-addressed storage, and personal agents have all become commodity at the same time — which is what makes the shape *implementable now* rather than only describable.
